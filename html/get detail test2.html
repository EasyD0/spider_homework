Abstract:
The anchors in wireless network localization system suffer from the risk of location leakage, which may result in some catastrophic dangers. To preserve the location privacy of anchors, this letter proposes to hide the anchor’s position in range-based localization through a secret sharing method. By decomposing the private input of anchors into several secret shares and concealing it in the constant term of the secret polynomial, the location information of anchors cannot be disclosed by any other node in the network including the server, during the entire localization process. The performance of the proposed scheme in privacy preservation, positioning accuracy and efficiency improvement (i.e., low communication and computation costs) are evaluated through simulations.

SECTION I.

Introduction

Location-based services (LBSs) now play an extremely important role in people’s daily activities, in which high-precision location is a prerequisite [1]. Although the global navigation satellite system (GNSS) can perform satisfactorily in most outdoor environments, they may fail to work indoors due to poor coverage or signal blockage.

As a good alternative, wireless network localization technique has received extensive research attention in recent years, and a great deal of related work has sprung up [2]. In such localization systems, an anchor network composed of nodes with known locations is generally used to provide reference information to help agents estimate their positions. The working principle of wireless network localization and the broadcast characteristics of wireless signals make the anchors prone to position leakage, which should be given full attention [3]. For example, the location information of incumbent radars in some military operations need to be preserved, because the disclosure of their positions may give the adversary opportunities to destroy them, resulting in devastating consequences [4]. For some civilian applications, the position privacy of anchors also need to be protected from some illegal attacks, to avoid unnecessary economic losses or privacy violations.

Several approaches have been proposed to protect nodes’ location privacy in wireless localization systems, e.g., differential privacy, information hiding [5], and k -anonymity. But these methods are mostly protection measures implemented by the system after it has obtained the node’s location. However, an absolutely reliable third party is often lacked in the majority of practical scenarios, that is exactly what privacy-preserving localization faces. To solve this, secure multi-party computing (MPC) as a tool to protect privacy information in the absence of a trusted third party has raised attentions in wireless localization [6]–​[10]. A privacy-preserving indoor localization scheme was presented in [6], in which the privacy of both the client’s location and the service provider’s database were protected via Paillier encryption and garbled circuits. The authors in [10] addressed the privacy issues of WiFi fingerprint-based localization through a Paillier cryptosystem. These works utilized the two-party computing (TPC) tool to protect privacy in the fingerprint-based localization. In such scenarios, only two parties are involved, typically the database server and the client, thus, TPC can effectively fit into them. However, in the range-based localization which contains more than three participants, the TPC methods may be restricted.

In this letter, we propose to protect the location privacy of anchors in the range-based localization through an MPC tool, i.e., secret sharing. In our proposed privacy-preserving localization scheme, the location information of each anchor is hidden from others, including other anchors, agents and server, during the entire positioning process. Simulations evaluate the localization accuracy, the communication and computation costs, and validate the privacy preservation and the efficiency improvement of the proposed scheme.

B. Shamir’s Secret Sharing

Secret sharing methods are cryptographic systems that protect secrets by decomposing them into multiple shares, of which Shamir’s secret sharing (SSS) is one of the most common threshold secret sharing methods. In short, the threshold secret sharing means that the secret can only be reconstructed by a certain number or more secret sharers. Specifically, we can define a scheme called (t , n )-threshold secret sharing, where n is the number of shares into which the secret is decomposed, and t is the threshold of the minimum number of staff required to reconstruct the secret. The (t , n )-threshold secret sharing cryptosystem allows any t (or more) participants to unlock the secret data, but any smaller group with less than t participants cannot retrieve the secret.

The detailed implementation process of the (t , n )-SSS cryptosystem is described as follows. If a secret holder denoted by H has a private input s , and s is from a finite field Fq , where q is a large prime. H distributes its secret into n separate shares via the method of secret polynomial in the following steps.

  • Assume there are n workers denoted by {wi},i=1,2,…,n , responsible for helping keep the secret for H . First, H randomly sets n different non-zero elements {xi}(i=1,2,…,n) and assigns xi to worker wi .

  • Then, H forms n different (t− 1)-degree polynomials as

    fi(s)=s+∑j=1t−1rjxji  mod q, i=1,2,…,n,(6)
    View Source\begin{equation*} f_{i}(s)=s+\sum _{j=1}^{t-1}r_{j}x_{i}^{j}~~{\mathrm {mod}}~q,~i=1,2, {\dots },n,\tag{6}\end{equation*} where coefficients {rj} are randomly chosen from Fq . The evaluated value of fi(s) is shared to corresponding worker wi .

By generating n polynomials and distributing them to different workers, H decomposes its secret into n shares and hides it as the constant term of each polynomial, i.e., s=fi(0),∀i .

From Lagrange interpolation rule, input s can be reconstructed by t or more workers with the corresponding polynomial values, which can be calculated by

s=fi(0)=∑i∈T∏j∈T,j≠i−xjxi−xjfi(s) mod q,(7)
View Source\begin{equation*} s=f_{i}(0)=\sum _{i\in \mathcal {T}}\prod _{j\in \mathcal {T},j\neq i}\frac {-x_{j}}{x_{i}-x_{j}}f_{i}(s)~{\mathrm {mod}}~q,\tag{7}\end{equation*} where T is the subset from n workers whose staff number is no less than t for retrieving the secret.

The SSS encryption scheme is homomorphic in addition and multiplication. Specifically, for inputs s1 and s2 , the shares at worker wi has the following properties:

C. Problem Formulation

In range-based wireless localization, each anchor provides information about the distance from the agent, along with its own location, to the platform/server. However, in practice, an absolutely reliable third party to guarantee the anchor’s location privacy is lacked. Hence, we will utilize the SSS tool to hide the anchors’ location from the server and other nodes while ensuring the correct calculation process of localization.

SECTION III.

Privacy-Preserving Localization via Shamir’s Secret Sharing

This section describes the specific implementation process of the proposed SSS-based encryption method.

A. Formula Transformation

The server calculates the positioning result for the agent through Eq. (3), in which the location information of each anchor, i.e., {pi} , is contained in matrix A and y. To protect the location privacy, the anchors are unwilling to directly send their raw location coordinates to the server. Meanwhile, the anchors in the network do not trust each other and will not disclose their own locations to the other nodes. Hence, the original calculation data cannot be covered up by entrusting one among the nodes to process the calculation results. Therefore, Eq. (3) needs to be transformed into the form that each anchor can deal with its information independently.

Next, the process of calculating the result of agent-position can be divided into two main steps: first, compute the value of ATA and take the inverse of it; second, compute the value of ATy and multiply it with the result of the previous step. It can be observed that, to do these two tasks, the server only needs to know some sum values rather than each individual parameter after the transformation in Eqs. (11) and (12). This is easy to implement in a system where the anchors belong to the same trust zone by appointing one of the anchors to collect the αiαTi ’s or αi(d2i−Ki) ’s of all the other nodes, sum them up and send it to the server. However, as mentioned above, the anchors are considered to distrust each other in this letter and will not release its location information to the others.

In the sequent subsection, we will elaborate how to utilize the SSS tool to design a privacy-preserving localization scheme, where each anchor i can separately evaluate the expressions related to their own information, i.e., αiαTi and αi(d2i−Ki) , and the server only gets the sum values, i.e., ATA and ATy .

SECTION IV.

Performance Analysis

This section analyzes the performance of the proposed SSS cryptographic scheme in protecting the location privacy of anchors in range-based localization system. Simulations are carried out on MATLAB platform of a laptop with an i7 processor and 16G memory. Assume five anchors are placed in a 50 m × 50 m field with one in each corner and one in the center. To be practical, the non-line-of-sight (NLOS) conditions are considered, in which the NLOS bias is modeled as an exponentially distributed variable with a mean of 2 ns. We first check the accuracy of localization results with the SSS-based scheme. Fig. 2 depicts the estimated results of 13 randomly placed agents. It can be shown that, although the NLOS propagations induce errors in the localization results, the SSS cryptosystem does not affect the estimation accuracy. This is a significant advantage over some encryption mechanisms based on adding noise that inevitably leads to the distortion of transmitted data and results in terrible positioning errors.

Next, we evaluate the superiority of the proposed SSS-based cryptosystem as an information-theoretic secure MPC protocol in communication and computation costs, compared to some computationally-secure ones, such as homomorphic encryption (HE). The Paillier encryption in [10] is provided for comparison. It can be seen from Fig. 3 that, the SSS-based method has exactly higher communication efficiency over that based on Paillier encryption. This is because the calculation of HE method is fully performed in the encrypted space, whose input has large bits, i.e., the ciphertext, which is much longer than the real number used in SSS encryption. At the same time, because the SSS method only needs some simple mathematical operations of real number, compared with the Paillier method, its computational complexity is very low and it only needs a very short execution time.

The communication and computation costs of two encryption schemes.

Compared with the HE method, our cryptosystem has another compelling merit. The confidentiality of HE method relies on the complexity of ciphertext making the adversary unable to decrypt within a limited time, it is computationally safe. As the computer performance improves, this scheme is not foolproof. While the SSS-based method is information-theoretically safe. According to Galois theory, as long as the number of colluding nodes does not exceed the predefined threshold, the real location of anchors will never be disclosed.

SECTION V.

Conclusion

In this letter, a privacy-preserving scheme based on the secret sharing method has been proposed, to hide the anchor’s position in the range-based localization system. The private input containing location information of each anchor has been concealed by the method of secret polynomial. Simulations have evaluated that the proposed encryption scheme is superior in high localization accuracy, low communication overhead, and low computation complexity.
